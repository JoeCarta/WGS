Colonialismo and the Puerto Rican Household

Joseph Cartafalsa

Timeline of US Colonialism in Puerto Rico

  • 1898

    US Invasion & Treaty of Paris

    Puerto Rico becomes a US colony after the Spanish-American War.

  • 1917

    Jones Act

    Puerto Ricans granted US citizenship; US controls shipping and trade, restricting economic autonomy.

  • 1937

    Law 116 & Mass Sterilization

    Sterilization promoted as population control and anti-poverty policy, targeting Puerto Rican women.

  • 1946–1964

    The Great Migration

    Hundreds of thousands of Puerto Ricans migrate to US cities, especially New York, due to economic hardship and US labor recruitment.

  • 1947–1970s

    Operation Bootstrap

    US-led industrialization: farms replaced by factories, mass migration, and economic restructuring.

  • 1976

    Section 936 Tax Incentives

    US tax breaks attract pharmaceutical companies, but profits flow out and dependency deepens.

  • 2006

    Section 936 Repealed

    Loss of tax incentives triggers economic crisis, mass unemployment, and deep recession.

  • 2016

    PROMESA & Fiscal Control Board

    US Congress imposes unelected board to manage Puerto Rico's finances, limiting local self-government.

  • Angela Davis

    Primitive Accumulation


    Women, Race, and Class, is comprised of essays analyzing the women’s liberation movement, slavery and racism in the United States, and a Marxist rigorous lens on economic and social factors that surround these movements and contexts. The 12th chapter specifically examines the relevance of racism in reproductive rights and vice versa. Davis explores how racist policy, particularly against women, has shaped reproductive rights and including public birth control norms. These were often based on eugenics or eugenics-adjacent ideas that were normalized by both societies at large and by corporations and legislators with power. She begins by noting the ways that this had affected women of color far differently than white women. Stating that “In New York, for instance, during the several years preceding the decriminalization of abortions in that state, some 80 percent of the deaths caused by illegal abortions involved Black and Puerto Rican women.” Further, she describes the policy of the United States to control populations and reproduction through women and women’s bodies as well as their households. She claims that “The domestic population policy of the U.S. government has an undeniably racist edge. Native American, Chicana, Puerto Rican and Black women continue to be sterilized in disproportionate numbers.” This not only provides a relevant and analogous lens to view US impositions on Puerto Ricans and Puerto Rican Americans, she approaches it precisely in her thesis and explorations.


    One of the specific objects of my analysis is directly referenced and evidenced by Davis. She describes the following, that “the astonishing number of Puerto Rican women who have been sterilized reflects a special government policy that can be traced back to 1939. In that year President Roosevelt’s Interdepartmental Committee on Puerto Rico issued a statement attributing the island’s economic problems to the phenomenon of overpopulation. This committee proposed that efforts be undertaken to reduce the birth rate to no more than the level of the death rate. Soon afterward an experimental sterilization campaign was undertaken in Puerto Rico.” This directly connects to both my historical analysis and the class discussions on reproductive control and rights and the increased har on women of color. This is further shows in Davis’s conclusion of the above context that “In this period over 150 birth control clinics were opened, resulting in a 20 percent decline in population growth by the mid-1960s. By the 1970s over 35 percent of all Puerto Rican women of childbearing age had been surgically sterilized.”


    Davis, In this same chapter, even touches on a topic that I would like to expand further on. She touches on the context of the US backed corporations and government planning that affected Puerto Rican households and the local jobs and households, as well as the economy, agriculture, and human geography. I realized that this reflects the analysis that I included of the US backed tax incentives for different industries, particularly pharmaceuticals. This incentive worked – initially – as pharmaceutical corporations moved in large numbers to open in Puerto Rico for labor and sales output. This industry ended amassing employment from a large amount of the population of locals, particularly ones already facing economic struggles or changes. Eventually, this tax holiday ended, and the pharmaceutical industry did not in fact stay to continue stimulating the economy, there was a mass economic exodus as soon as the tax holiday ended and many were left unemployed. This is just a specific example of the themes of topography and the way economie control households and people, particularly groups who have been made into minorities. Davis connects this to her own analysis, she states that “During the 1970s the devastating implications of the Puerto Rican experiment began to emerge with unmistakable clarity. In Puerto Rico the presence of corporations in the highly automated metallurgical and pharmaceutical industries had exacerbated the problem of unemployment. The prospect of an ever-larger army of unemployed workers was one of the main incentives for the mass sterilization program.” I plan on looking further into this in conversation with the Caliban and the Witch.

  • Angela Davis

    Primitive Accumulation


    In Caliban and the Witch, Federici describes how capitalism, beyond the common analysis of labor and production, has deeply rooted in taking control of bodies, especially women’s, and turning them into instruments of production. She writes that “The body has been for women in capitalist society what the factory has been for male waged workers... appropriated by the state and men and forced to function as a means for the reproduction and accumulation of labor.” That line could just as easily describe what happened in Puerto Rico under U.S. colonial policy. Women’s bodies, specifically their reproductive capacity, were treated as something to be managed, regulated, and ultimately controlled for the benefit of capital and empire.
    Angela Davis documents how over 35% of Puerto Rican women of childbearing age were sterilized by the 1970s, many through coercion or without proper consent. This wasn’t accidental, it ties directly to U.S. population policy, which framed Puerto Rico’s economic struggles as a problem of “overpopulation.” As Federici notes, “This process required the transformation of the body into a work-machine, and the subjugation of women to the reproduction of the work-force.” Sterilization became a tool of economic restructuring, aimed at managing a surplus labor force that U.S.-backed industries, like the pharmaceutical companies incentivized to relocate to the island, had no intention of permanently employing. This furthers analysis of the idea – previously states – that once the tax breaks expired, the companies left, and Puerto Ricans were left with fewer jobs and even fewer rights over their own households. Federici’s argument that colonization required not just labor extraction but the destruction of social power, especially that of women, shows up clearly in the Puerto Rican case. The attack on reproductive autonomy mirrors what she calls “the extermination of the ‘witches’” in Europe, a campaign not just against individuals but against entire forms of knowledge, autonomy, and communal resistance.
    Another description I noticed in the Caliban and the Witch actually came from a source she had cited in her novel in describing the Taino (Native Caribbean) populations encountered by Columbus and Europeans, this is idea and the citation as well is brought in by her in the beginning of the chapter The Accumulation of Labor and the Degradation of Women. She also draws a parallel between the ideological violence of the early colonial period and ongoing justifications for domination. The image of the peaceful Taino and the “savage” Carib, as Federici cites from Retamar, didn’t exist as simple racial myths, they were tools to rationalize extermination, “Both the gentle Tainos and the ferocious Caribs were exterminated... Each image corresponds to a colonial intervention—assuming its right to control the lives of the aboriginal population.” This logic carried forward in time and society, while (and well after) colonial states framed native populations as either innocent dependents or threats to be neutralized. For Puerto Rican women, this meant being cast either by 20th centruy US ideas as welfare burdens or irresponsible reproducers, both roles justifying outside control of their bodies. That being, Federici reveals another logical perspective behind Davis’s historical account that reproductive violence in Puerto Rico wasn’t an isolated policy failure, more accurately it can be said to be a part of a broader capitalist project of bodily control. The colonial state treated the female body as territory where it aimed for it to be conquered, regulated, and put to work; not only for its own population’s labor needs, but for the expansion and preservation of global capital.

  • Angela Davis

    Primitive Accumulation


    Kimberlé Crenshaw’s concept of intersectionality helps explain why Puerto Rican women have been disproportionately affected by U.S. colonial policies. Crenshaw argues that when systems of power ignore overlapping identities, people at the margins fall through the cracks. She states, “If you're standing in the path of multiple forms of exclusion, you're likely to get hit by both.” This is exactly what happened in Puerto Rico, where women were not only targeted by population control efforts like mass sterilization but also faced racialized economic exploitation. These women were subjects of both gendered and colonial control. As Crenshaw says, “If we can't see a problem, we can't fix it.” Analysing Puerto Rican reproductive injustice includeds looking at how racism, sexism, and colonialism can all collectively affect real people who happen to be born into certain groupings, and this is often ignored or even simply sympathized with rather than actually approached.
